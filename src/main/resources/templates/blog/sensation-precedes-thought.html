One of the political issues that I've always been interested in, is abortion. Perhaps not because of the issue in and of itself, but because of what it represents. It may be the best example of a phenomenon that absolutely fascinates me, that which makes politics a thing in the first place: two people, looking at the same set of facts, and coming to radically different conclusions.

I'll never tire of it. Bravery, or foolishness? Cautious, or cowardly? Discipline, or cruelty? Love, or enablement? Exciting, or terrifying? I feel these differences in sensibilities drive almost all political issues, and abortion is somewhat of the textbook example.

And recently, I've started to question where these sensibilities come from. Why does one person come to believe that abortion is a woman's personal choice, and another that abortion is the murder of an innocent life? What drives these frames of reference? Did they just hear a convincing argument one day and decide, “Well, that does it, I'm pro-this now!” No, that seems off.

I cannot speak for anyone else, but introspection into my feelings about abortion revealed that I simply had an intuition about the subject. It's as if the issue simply found its way through my brain tissue, and eventually something just clicked, some spark ignited, and a flame of passion decided where I would take my stand (which I won't mention because that's a whole different story).

Is this how it works for everyone else? If so, where does this intuition come from? The straightforward answer is: it was inherited, and then honed through experience during our upbringings.

...but then you start to think about it more, and that's where the fun (?) begins.

You could've been born to anyone. You're a victim — or a beneficiary, if you're lucky — of your parents. You are a result, and the obvious hints are your physical attributes. It doesn't have to be exact, and there are exceptions, but generally, your size, your appearance, your skin color... all about the same. Mental attributes are affected too. Your personality, your beliefs, your values... much of them are inherited, too. One interesting example of this, is how children often inherit their parents' political leanings.

“Nuture” also leaves its impression. Consider the variables that go into a person's upbringing. Where are they born, what country? Is it a first-world country, a second-world, a third-world? What culture are they born into? Someone growing up in the Middle East is going to have different experiences compared to someone growing up in Western Europe. And consider time as well. Growing up in modern-day, 2023 Germany is going to be quite different compared to growing up during the years of you-know-who.

Moreso, consider this: nuture is inextricably tied to nature, because any experiences you have growing up are interpreted through your inherited lens, i.e. the not-so-blank slate of a brain that your parents have given you.

What are we then? What do we really see with our eyes, what do we really hear with our ears? We see feelings, and we hear feelings. Feelings crafted by nature and nuture, churned out by biological machinery in the brain, and then we rationalize these feelings and they become thought and ideas.

The other way around simply seems too strange. Do we really just sit around and think about issues, and then decide that X is good and Y is bad? No, we have instinctual leanings, we understand inherently that some things are right and some things are wrong.

And now we return to the issue of abortion. It so “undebatable,” it's straight-up axiomatic. Sensation drives the issue from the very root and nothing else matters. Either you feel for the unborn, or you don't. You fall in line with where the universe deems you should be.

I've read about various pro-life and pro-choice arguments, such as the famed violinist argument, but all the arguments seem pointless if one considers that “sensation precedes thought.” Then they're all just rationalizations, trying to think our way through our amygdalas' whims.

And yet, somewhat ironically, I feel hesistant to fully buy into this idea. It has a lot of ramifications, and I think it's closely related to the idea of free will and determinism, and I don't feel confident enough to step into that territory.

The laws of physics, the nature of the universe, the existence of God — so many things go into the free-will debate. I regret that I have neither the knowledge nor expertise to explore that, because I think that's where my ramblings ultimately lead to: if sensation precedes thought, then what does that imply about free will?
